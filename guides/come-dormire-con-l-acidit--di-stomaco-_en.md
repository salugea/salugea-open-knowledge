## Stomach acidity: how many pillows to use for sleeping

One of the most frequent questions among those suffering from **stomach acidity is: how many pillows should I use** to sleep well without annoying acid regurgitation?

If you are wondering this too, here is the answer!

One of the simplest and most effective strategies to sleep better in case of stomach acidity is to **use two pillows to support the head**. This raised position helps to counteract the rising of gastric acids towards the esophagus during the night. That is why it is recommended.

Keeping the torso slightly raised exploits the effect of gravity, which helps keep acids inside the stomach, reducing their impact on the esophageal mucosa and limiting annoying manifestations during the night.

You must know that the esophagus is a kind of "tube" that connects the mouth to the stomach and that its mucosa is less "resistant" to acids than that of the stomach. That is why, when gastric acids rise along it, they can create discomfort, such as burning and acidity.

**If you find sleeping with two pillows uncomfortable**, you can opt for an **inclined pillow** (wedge pillow) designed specifically for this purpose. Alternatively, if you have an adjustable bed, you could slightly tilt the upper part to achieve the same result.

## Right or left side? Which to choose to sleep with acidity

Another common question among those suffering from stomach acidity is: **on which side is it better to sleep to avoid stomach acids rising?**

It is often recommended to sleep on the **left side**, as this position helps the lower esophageal sphincter (a sort of "valve") to function better, reducing the risk of gastric acids rising into the esophagus.

Conversely, sleeping on the **right side** could worsen the problem because pressure on the stomach is increased, favoring the rising of gastric content.

### Why stress is the enemy of sleep and digestion

When we are stressed, our body enters "alarm mode". In very simple words, it prepares to act and react: a situation that is perceived as danger.

This activates a series of physiological processes, including an increase in the production of hormones like cortisol.

Cortisol, particularly when its levels are altered, and stress can negatively affect both gastric and emotional well-being. For example, they can contribute to:

* stimulating the stomach to produce **more acids** than necessary;
* slowing down digestion, causing food to remain in the stomach longer and increasing the risk of reflux;
* making falling asleep more difficult and interfering with sleep quality.

This is why it is important to try to keep stress levels under control to support digestive well-being and good rest.
